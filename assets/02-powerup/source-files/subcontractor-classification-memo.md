## Purpose

This memo provides guidance on properly classifying workers as employees or independent contractors (subcontractors). Misclassification is one of the most common compliance violations in the construction industry and carries significant penalties.

---

## Why It Matters

Misclassifying an employee as an independent contractor can result in:

- **Back taxes** — Employer must pay unpaid FICA, FUTA, and income tax withholding
- **Penalties** — IRS penalties of 1.5% to 3% of wages plus 100% of the unpaid employee FICA
- **Workers' comp liability** — Uninsured claims for injuries to misclassified workers
- **State penalties** — NC, SC, and VA all have state-level misclassification enforcement
- **Lawsuits** — Misclassified workers may sue for benefits, overtime, and other protections they were denied

---

## IRS 20-Factor Test (Common Law Test)

The IRS evaluates the degree of control and independence across three categories:

### Behavioral Control

Does the company control *how* the work is done?

| Factor | Employee | Contractor |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Instructions | Company directs when, where, and how to work | Worker controls methods |
| Training | Company provides training | Worker uses own methods |
| Work sequence | Company sets the order of work | Worker determines sequence |

### Financial Control

Does the company control the *business aspects* of the work?

| Factor | Employee | Contractor |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Investment | Company provides tools and equipment | Worker invests in own tools |
| Expenses | Company reimburses expenses | Worker bears own expenses |
| Payment | Regular wages (hourly/salary) | Flat fee or per-project |
| Profit/Loss | No opportunity for profit or loss | Can profit or lose money |
| Market | Works for one company | Available to the market |

### Relationship Type

What is the *nature of the relationship*?

| Factor | Employee | Contractor |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Contract | Implied or formal employment agreement | Written contractor agreement |
| Benefits | Eligible for benefits | No benefits |
| Permanence | Ongoing, indefinite | Project-based, defined scope |
| Key services | Work is core to the business | Work is supplementary |

## DOL Economic Reality Test

The Department of Labor uses a different test focused on economic dependence:

1. **Opportunity for profit or loss** depending on managerial skill
2. **Investments** by the worker and the employer
3. **Degree of permanence** of the relationship
4. **Nature and degree of control** by the employer
5. **Extent to which the work is integral** to the employer's business
6. **Skill and initiative** of the worker

No single factor is determinative. The totality of circumstances governs.

## Construction-Specific Red Flags

These situations commonly indicate misclassification in construction:

- Worker shows up daily at the same time, uses company tools, and follows company safety protocols → likely **employee**
- Worker bids on projects, carries own insurance, serves multiple clients, and sets own schedule → likely **contractor**
- "1099 crew" that works exclusively for one general contractor, wears company shirts, and is supervised by company foremen → almost certainly **misclassified employees**

### Common Misclassification Patterns

1. **Labeling for convenience** — Calling workers "subcontractors" to avoid payroll taxes and workers' comp
2. **Control without classification** — Directing every aspect of the work but paying on a 1099
3. **Verbal agreements** — No written contract defining scope, deliverables, or independence
4. **Sole-source contractors** — Workers who only work for one company and have no other clients

## State-Specific Notes

### North Carolina

NC uses the IRS common law test. The NC Department of Revenue actively audits construction companies for misclassification. Penalties include back taxes plus interest.

### South Carolina

SC follows federal guidelines. The SC Department of Employment and Workforce investigates misclassification complaints. Construction companies are frequent audit targets.

### Virginia

Virginia enacted the Worker Misclassification Act (2020). Key provisions:
- Creates a specific penalty structure for misclassification
- Allows workers to file complaints with the Department of Labor and Industry
- Debarment from public contracts for repeat offenders

## What to Do

### Before Engaging a Worker

1. Determine classification *before* work begins
2. Document the basis for the classification decision
3. Execute a written agreement that reflects the actual relationship
4. Verify the worker's insurance (GL, workers' comp if they have employees)

### If You're Unsure

- File IRS Form SS-8 (Determination of Worker Status) for an IRS opinion
- Consult with employment counsel
- Default to classifying as an employee — the penalties for misclassifying an employee as a contractor are far worse than the cost of payroll taxes
